## Setting

You smell the garlic before you see the menu: not aggressive, but honest, the way a place signals it is not chasing trends. The dining room is small—brick, low beams, tables close enough to hear your neighbors fall in love with the bread. Service moves with the calm rhythm of people who have done this Tuesday a hundred times and still mean it.

## The meal

The antipasti plate is a quiet flex: mortadella sliced thin enough to read through, olives warmed just enough to release their oil, and a soft cheese that tastes like pasture instead of packaging. The ragù arrives in a shallow bowl, wide enough to cool the pasta at the edges while the center stays hot—a practical detail that also reads as care. Noodles are slightly shy of al dente in the best way: yielding, but not sloppy.

Dessert was a simple olive oil cake with citrus. It did not photograph like a trophy. It ate like a last paragraph that lands.

## Verdict

Warm Hearth is not reinventing Italian food. It is reminding you why the canon became canon. I would return on a rainy night without checking the menu first—and for a critic, that is its own recommendation.
